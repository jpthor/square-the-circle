Squaring the circle, approximately

Exact squaring is impossible — π is transcendental (Lindemann, 1882). The 340-year sport is approximate squaring: with compass and straightedge, construct a segment whose square matches the circle's area to as many digits as possible, in as few strokes as possible. This page replays every construction live: each figure is recomputed from raw intersections in your browser, and the digits shown are computed, not quoted.

The problem

Given a circle, construct — using only an unmarked straightedge and a compass — a square of exactly the same area. Posed by the Greeks, it resisted for over two millennia until Lindemann proved in 1882 that π is transcendental: no finite chain of lines and circles can produce the required length √π·r, because every constructible length is algebraic.

What survives is the approximation game. A construction earns its keep on two axes: how many correct digits of π its square captures, and how few strokes it needs (one stroke = one line through two constructed points, or one circle with a constructed centre; intersections are free). Every entry below is a point on that trade-off — and the chart above shows the 2026 state of play.

Two flavours of compass appear throughout: Euclid's collapsing compass (a circle may only be drawn about one constructed point through another) and the modern rigid compass (a distance may be carried elsewhere, one stroke). The compass equivalence theorem makes them equal in power — but not in stroke counts, which is why "transfer-free" constructions carry a badge here.

A short history

c. 1650 BC
Rhind papyrus
Egyptian scribes square the circle with side 8d/9 — implying π ≈ 3.1605. One digit.
c. 450 BC
Anaxagoras
Works on the problem while imprisoned in Athens — the first recorded attempt at the exact problem.
c. 440 BC
Hippocrates of Chios
Squares his lunes — the first curved regions ever squared exactly, feeding two millennia of false hope.
c. 250 BC
Archimedes
Brackets π between 223/71 and 22/7, and shows squaring the circle is equivalent to rectifying it.
c. 480 AD
Zu Chongzhi
Finds the miracle fraction 355/113 (the Milü) — π to 6.6 digits, unbeatable until 5-digit denominators.
1685
Adam Kochański
Rectifies the circumference to 4 decimal places with famously few moves — one compass opening. (Builder in the next tab.)
1849
Jacob de Gelder
Constructs 355/113 as a length via 3 + 4²/(7²+8²) — but never the square's side. (Builder in the next tab.)
1882
Ferdinand von Lindemann
Proves π transcendental. The exact problem dies; the approximation sport is all that remains.
1913
Srinivasa Ramanujan
Constructs the side √(355/113)·r — off by one inch on a 140,000-square-mile circle. (Builder in the next tab.)
1914
Ramanujan again
Reaches 8 decimal places via (9²+19²/22)^¼, riding a freak spike (16539) in the continued fraction of π⁴.
1913–2019
Hobson, Dixon, Chu
Hobson gets the side in very few moves at ~4 digits; Chu (2019) reaches 9 digits at 68+ steps.
2022
Frédéric Beatrix
Geometrography survey (Parabola 58(2)): 13 steps → 4.32 digits, the published simplicity record.
2026
This project
Machine-audited 355/113 constructions (14, 15 strokes) — then a computer search collapses the frontier: 5 strokes → 6.2 digits, 7 strokes → 8.0 digits, transfer-free. See the New solutions tab.

What the computer search changed

Three search engines (an exhaustive stroke-space BFS, 290k random rollouts over a 3.4-million-point catalog, and a ~30-million-state beam search) showed that record-beating constructions are not rare treasures but lie around at almost every stroke count — because approximate squaring has always been coincidence-hunting, and the published record simply reflected how small a space humans could search by hand. The mined constants have no number-theoretic meaning; the constructions that mean something (355/113, (2143/22)^¼) owe their accuracy to giant terms in continued fractions, and tellingly the unbiased search never stumbled on 4/√113 at any depth: meaningful constructions must be aimed at, record-breaking ones are lying around.

Caveats that travel with the results: the 4-stroke uses a tangency point; the 5-, 8- and 9-stroke need a rigid compass (their strict-Euclid counts are larger); the Ramanujan 1913 stroke range rests on an unverified compilation; and every construction here produces the square's side, not the drawn square.
